I wanted to wear something from space. Not a novelty item from a gift shop — something I made myself, from a piece of iron that spent four billion years drifting through the solar system before crashing into northern Sweden.

This is the story of that ring.

## The Material

**Muonionalusta** is an iron meteorite found near the Muonio River in Sweden. It's classified as type IVA — mostly iron and nickel — and it's famous for its **Widmanstätten patterns**: geometric crystal structures that only form over millions of years of extremely slow cooling in space.

You can't replicate these patterns on Earth. They're the fingerprint of deep time.

I sourced a small slab (about 40mm × 30mm × 4mm) from a verified dealer. It arrived wrapped in oil paper, dark grey, unremarkable. All the beauty was hiding inside.

## The Process

### 1. Cutting the Blank

Using a jeweler's saw with a fine blade, I cut a strip from the slab. Meteorite iron is *tough* — much harder than regular steel due to the nickel content. I broke two blades before finding the right pressure.

### 2. Forming the Band

I annealed the strip with a torch, then slowly bent it around a steel mandrel. Meteorite iron doesn't like to bend. It cracks if you rush it. Three heating cycles, gentle taps with a rawhide mallet, and eventually I had a rough circle.

### 3. The Damascus Liner

For structural support and contrast, I forged a Damascus steel liner from alternating layers of 1095 and 15N20 steel. 200+ layers, folded and welded. This forms the inner band that actually sits on the finger.

### 4. Etching the Pattern

This is where the magic happens. I submerged the meteorite surface in a warm ferric chloride solution for about 90 seconds. The acid eats away at the iron and nickel at different rates, revealing those impossible geometric patterns.

Watching the Widmanstätten structure appear for the first time was genuinely emotional. This pattern formed before Earth had oceans.

### 5. Assembly and Finish

I bonded the meteorite inlay to the Damascus band with aerospace epoxy, finished the edges with progressively finer sandpaper (up to 2000 grit), and sealed the meteorite with a micro-crystalline wax to prevent oxidation.

## The Result

The ring weighs 14 grams. On the outside, you see the etched Widmanstätten pattern — silver-white geometric lines against a dark iron background. On the inside, the Damascus steel pattern ripples like water.

It's not perfect. There's a small void near the seam where a nickel inclusion fell out during etching. I'm keeping it. Imperfection is part of the story.

## What I Learned

Working with meteorite taught me patience in a way that software never has. You can't `Ctrl+Z` a cracked meteorite slab. Every cut is permanent. Every decision matters.

And every time I look at my hand, I'm reminded that the atoms in this ring were forged in a star that died before our sun was born. That's worth a few broken saw blades.
